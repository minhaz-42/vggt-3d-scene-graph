\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

All numbers are object-label F1 against the independent 28-scene reference of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} (multiset precision, recall, and F1 of fused-node labels, mean over scenes
at $3/5/8/10$ input views). Section~\ref{subsec:decirc} contrasts this with the earlier
prediction-seeded pseudo-reference.

\subsection{Main Comparison}
\label{subsec:variants}

Table~\ref{tab:variant_f1} compares the seven variants of Section~\ref{sec:exp_variants}. On real,
detector-grounded labels \textsc{graph-fusion} is the strongest prior fusion variant, yet object-label
F1 \emph{falls} as views are added for every cross-view fusion variant: more views supply more
detections of the same physical object, which fusion does not collapse, so precision degrades against a
fixed reference. The non-fusion references \textsc{2d-only} and \textsc{geometry-only} plateau.
\textsc{graph-fusion-dedup} is the only variant whose accuracy \emph{rises} toward the dense end, and is
best at every view count (Figure~\ref{fig:results}).

\subsection{Duplicate-Aware Fusion: the Positive Result}
\label{subsec:dedup}

\input{figures/fig_dedup3d}

Duplicate-aware fusion adds a single post-fusion step: same-label fused nodes whose axis-aligned 3D
boxes overlap (IoU $>\tau$, default $\tau{=}0.1$) are merged and re-fused, collapsing the duplicate
nodes of one physical object into a single object (Figure~\ref{fig:dedup3d}). Dedup raises object-label
F1 at every view count, by $+0.057$ to $+0.122$ over \textsc{graph-fusion} and by $+0.065$ to $+0.154$
over the uninformed \textsc{fixed-shrink} control, and the gain \emph{grows} with views, as expected for a
step removing the duplicate detections that accumulate as more frames are seen. It wins on $22$ to $27$ of
the $28$ scenes at every view count (two-sided sign test $p<0.001$; per-view deltas and per-scene W/L/T in
Table~S4, supplementary). The improvement holds on the $22$ static scenes ($+0.082$ to $+0.171$), the $6$
dynamic scenes with a moving person ($+0.060$ to $+0.116$), and the $5$ human-verified gold scenes alone
($+0.068/+0.085/+0.142/+0.146$), so it is not an artifact of the VLM-drafted references. The gain is
robust to the merge threshold, holding in the $+0.075$ to $+0.118$ range (average $\Delta$) across
$\tau\in[0,0.2]$ and fading only for strict $\tau$ ($+0.030$ at $\tau\ge0.5$); a centroid-distance term
does not help, so 3D-box IoU alone suffices (Fig.~S2, supplementary).

\subsection{Uncertainty-Aware Fusion: the Negative Result}
\label{subsec:uncertainty}

We treat the uncertainty-aware fusion gate, the headline of an earlier framing of this work, as an
ablation (full numbers in Table~S3, supplementary). The recalibrated \textsc{proposed} variant is
\emph{worse} at every view count ($-0.033$ to $-0.096$ vs.\ \textsc{graph-fusion}) and loses on
essentially every scene ($26$ to $27$ of $28$ at v5--v10, sign test $p<0.001$). A sweep over the
uncertainty weight $w \in \{0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$ does not rescue it: \emph{no} weight in
$[0.1, 0.8]$ beats \textsc{graph-fusion} at any view count, with larger weights worse at the dense end.
The result survives reference filtering: dropping \texttt{unknown object} (which the open-vocabulary
detector never predicts) or the ``stuff'' classes (wall/floor/ceiling) raises every variant's F1 but
leaves the ranking unchanged.

The negative result is informative, not a mere null: the gate does what it was designed to do, lifting
recall (mean \textsc{proposed} $0.516/0.556/0.596/0.602$ vs.\ \textsc{graph-fusion}
$0.510/0.540/0.581/0.587$) but fragmenting correct objects and costing more precision than it buys
($0.478/0.388/0.299/0.266$ vs.\ $0.556/0.494/0.420/0.399$), so net F1 is lower. This is the
\emph{opposite} axis to duplicate-aware fusion, which merges \emph{more} where the system over-splits
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Per-Class Characterization: the Over-Counting Signature}
\label{subsec:perclass}

Per-class micro-averages (Tables~S1 and~S2, supplementary; dropping \texttt{unknown object}, which the
detector never emits) localize the effect. For \textsc{graph-fusion} recall rises from $0.700$ at three
views to $0.802$ at ten while precision falls from $0.519$ to $0.331$, so F1 peaks at the sparse end
($0.596$ at v3, $0.468$ at v10): more frames find more objects but emit more duplicates, and the
precision loss dominates. It concentrates in classes \emph{found reliably but over-counted} (recall near
$1.0$, precision collapsing), such as \texttt{desk} ($0.15$), \texttt{floor} ($0.27$), \texttt{monitor}
($0.43$), and \texttt{chair} ($0.45$), whereas distinct, well-separated objects (\texttt{book},
\texttt{bottle}, \texttt{cup}) fuse cleanly.

Dedup at v8 confirms the mechanism, recovering precision on exactly the over-counted classes
(\texttt{desk} $0.15\to0.45$, \texttt{floor} $0.27\to0.71$, \texttt{monitor} $0.43\to0.64$,
\texttt{chair} $0.45\to0.71$) while leaving recall unchanged for almost every class; its only recall
cost is $-0.04$ on closely-packed same-class instances (\texttt{desk}, \texttt{bottle}), which the
precision gain dwarfs. The dominant error is over-counting of correctly-found objects, not mislabeling,
precisely what duplicate-aware fusion targets; separating closely-packed instances by appearance is left
to future work (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{De-Circularization: the Earlier Pseudo-Reference Was Misleading}
\label{subsec:decirc}

Both findings are visible only under an independent reference. Seeding the reference from the model's own
ten-view \textsc{graph-fusion} output produces two artifacts: \textsc{graph-fusion} scores $1.0$ at ten
views \emph{by construction} (the reference is its own prediction), and the uncertainty variant, which
splits more, reproduces more of the reference's fragments and looks like a sparse-view \emph{win}
($+0.040$/$+0.021$ at v3/v5). Fixing the object count independently reverses the uncertainty result and
removes the dense-view $1.0$ artifact (Tables~S1 and~S3, supplementary). A prediction-seeded reference is
an easy, silent way to inflate sparse-view 3D scores, and it masked both findings.
