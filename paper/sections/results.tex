\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

All numbers are object-label F1 against the independent 28-scene reference of
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} (multiset precision/recall/F1 of fused-node labels, mean over scenes at
$3/5/8/10$ input views). The contrast with the earlier prediction-seeded pseudo-reference is in
Section~\ref{subsec:decirc}.

\subsection{Variant comparison on the independent reference}
\label{subsec:variants}

Table~\ref{tab:variant_f1} compares the seven variants of Section~\ref{sec:exp_variants}. On real,
detector-grounded labels \textsc{graph-fusion} is the strongest prior fusion variant, yet object-label
F1 \emph{falls} as views are added for every cross-view fusion variant: more views supply more
detections of the same physical object, which fusion does not collapse, so precision degrades against a
fixed reference (the non-fusion references \textsc{2d-only} and \textsc{geometry-only} merely plateau).
\textsc{graph-fusion-dedup} is the only variant whose accuracy \emph{rises} toward the dense end and is
best at every view count (Figure~\ref{fig:results}).

\subsection{Duplicate-aware fusion: the positive result}
\label{subsec:dedup}

\input{figures/fig_dedup3d}

Duplicate-aware fusion adds a single post-fusion step: same-label fused nodes whose axis-aligned 3D
boxes overlap (IoU $> \tau$, default $\tau{=}0.1$) are merged and re-fused. Table~\ref{tab:dedup_delta}
reports the gain over both the \textsc{graph-fusion} baseline and the uninformed \textsc{fixed-shrink}
control. Dedup improves object-label F1 at every view count by $+0.057$ to $+0.122$ over
\textsc{graph-fusion} and by $+0.065$ to $+0.154$ over \textsc{fixed-shrink}; the gain \emph{grows}
with views, as expected for a step that removes the duplicate detections that accumulate as more
frames are seen. It wins on $22$--$27$ of $28$ scenes at every view count (two-sided sign test
$p<0.001$). The improvement holds on both the $22$ static scenes ($+0.082$ to $+0.171$) and the $6$
dynamic scenes with a moving person ($+0.060$ to $+0.116$), and on the $5$ human-verified gold scenes
alone ($+0.068/+0.089/+0.145/+0.146$), so it is not an artifact of the VLM-drafted references.

\subsection{Uncertainty-aware fusion: a negative result}
\label{subsec:uncertainty}

We treat the uncertainty-aware fusion gate --- the headline of an earlier framing of this work --- as
an ablation. Table~\ref{tab:uncertainty_neg} reports its delta against \textsc{graph-fusion}. The
recalibrated \textsc{proposed} variant is \emph{worse} at every view count ($-0.033$ to $-0.096$) and
loses on essentially every scene ($26$--$27$ of $28$ at v5--v10; sign test $p<0.001$). A sweep over the
uncertainty weight $w \in \{0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8\}$ does not rescue it: \emph{no} weight in
$[0.1, 0.8]$ beats \textsc{graph-fusion} at any view count, with larger weights worse at the dense end.
The result is robust to reference filtering: dropping \texttt{unknown object} (which the
open-vocabulary detector never predicts) or the ``stuff'' classes (wall/floor/ceiling) raises every
variant's F1 but leaves the ranking unchanged.

The mechanism is informative rather than a mere null. The uncertainty modulation does exactly what it
was designed to do --- it lifts recall by declining to merge uncertain proposals (full-multiset recall,
mean over scenes: \textsc{proposed} $0.516/0.556/0.596/0.602$ vs.\ \textsc{graph-fusion}
$0.510/0.540/0.581/0.587$) --- but the same behavior fragments correct objects and costs more precision
than the recall it buys (precision: \textsc{proposed} $0.478/0.388/0.299/0.266$ vs.\ \textsc{graph-fusion}
$0.556/0.495/0.420/0.399$). Against a fixed-count reference the net F1 is lower --- the
\emph{opposite} axis to duplicate-aware fusion, which merges \emph{more} where the system over-splits
(we develop this contrast in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Per-class characterization: the over-counting signature}
\label{subsec:perclass}

Table~\ref{tab:perclass_overall} reports the micro-averaged precision/recall/F1 of \textsc{graph-fusion}
per view count (dropping \texttt{unknown object}, which the detector never emits). The signature is
clear: recall rises from $0.700$ at three views to $0.802$ at ten, while precision falls from $0.519$
to $0.331$, so net F1 is best at the sparse end ($0.596$ at v3, $0.468$ at v10). The system finds more
true objects with more frames but emits more duplicate detections, and the precision loss dominates.

Per class (at v8), this splits into three behaviors. A group is \emph{found reliably but over-counted}
--- recall near $1.0$ while precision collapses: \texttt{desk} ($0.15$ precision), \texttt{wall}
($0.26$), \texttt{floor} ($0.27$), \texttt{keyboard} ($0.39$), \texttt{monitor} ($0.43$), \texttt{chair}
($0.45$). These are always detected, but the 3D fusion does not collapse the several detections of one
physical object, making them the dominant precision sink. A second group is \emph{clean wins} with high
precision and recall (\texttt{book}, \texttt{bottle}, \texttt{picture}, \texttt{person}, \texttt{cup}):
distinct, well-separated objects that fuse correctly. A third group is \emph{under-detected}:
\texttt{cabinet} (recall $0.27$), where the detector under-fires on blurry, low-resolution TUM frames.
The dominant error is thus over-counting of correctly-found objects, not mislabeling --- precisely what
duplicate-aware fusion targets (Figure~\ref{fig:perclass}).

Table~\ref{tab:dedup_mechanism} confirms the mechanism at v8: dedup sharply raises precision on the
over-counted classes (\texttt{desk} $0.15 \to 0.45$, \texttt{floor} $0.27 \to 0.71$, \texttt{monitor}
$0.43 \to 0.64$, \texttt{cabinet} $0.36 \to 0.67$, \texttt{chair} $0.45 \to 0.71$) while leaving recall
unchanged for almost every class. The only recall cost is on closely-packed same-class instances ---
\texttt{desk} and \texttt{bottle} lose $-0.04$ recall, as adjacent same-class objects with overlapping
axis-aligned 3D boxes are merged. The net is a large F1 gain because the precision recovery dwarfs the
recall cost; separating closely-packed same-class instances by appearance is left to future work
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Robustness of the duplicate-aware merge}
\label{subsec:dedup_robust}

The dedup gain is robust to the IoU threshold (Table~\ref{tab:dedup_iou}): the improvement over
\textsc{graph-fusion} stays in the $+0.075$ to $+0.118$ range (average $\Delta$) across the low-IoU band
$\tau \in [0, 0.2]$ and only fades once $\tau$ is strict enough to stop merging duplicates
($\tau \ge 0.5$, $+0.030$). The default $\tau{=}0.1$ is within noise of the optimum; a
centroid-distance OR-term does not help, so 3D-box IoU alone is the right and simplest criterion.

\subsection{De-circularization: the earlier pseudo-reference was misleading}
\label{subsec:decirc}

The two findings above are visible only under an independent reference. The earlier evaluation seeded
the reference from the model's own ten-view \textsc{graph-fusion} output, producing two artifacts.
\emph{(i)} \textsc{graph-fusion} scored $1.0$ at ten views \emph{by construction} --- the reference
\emph{was} its own prediction. \emph{(ii)} The uncertainty-aware variant, which keeps more proposals
unmerged and so splits more, reproduced more of the fragments that reference contained and appeared to
be a sparse-view \emph{win} ($+0.040$/$+0.021$ at v3/v5). Fixing the true object count independently
penalizes over-splitting: the uncertainty result reverses sign (Table~\ref{tab:uncertainty_neg}) and
the dense-view $1.0$ artifact disappears (F1 falls with views, Table~\ref{tab:perclass_overall}). A
prediction-seeded reference is an easy, silent way to inflate scores in sparse-view 3D, and it masked
both of this paper's empirical findings.
