\section{Introduction}

\input{figures/fig_teaser}

Reconstructing a scene and explaining a scene are different goals. Feed-forward geometry models now
recover camera motion, depth, and point maps directly from a few
images~\cite{wang2024dust3r,leroy2024mast3r,wang2025vggt}, and 2D foundation models supply
open-vocabulary detections, masks, and
descriptors~\cite{minderer2023owlv2,radford2021clip,kirillov2023segment,oquab2023dinov2}. Yet
downstream applications want object-level statements --- which pixels across views are the same object,
where it is in 3D, and how it relates to others (``the monitor is on the desk'') --- and these
components do not by themselves define such a graph, especially from a handful of casual RGB views
rather than a calibrated RGB-D stream, dense video, or a prebuilt map.

We construct the graph as evidence-preserving fusion over lifted 2D proposals, with all heavy models
frozen and no per-scene optimization: VGGT world-point maps act as a 2D-to-3D lookup, an
open-vocabulary detector proposes labeled boxes, and these are lifted and fused across views into a
labeled object--relation graph (Figure~\ref{fig:teaser}). This keeps perception fixed while making the
graph stage explicit and easy to ablate, and targets a lower-input regime than open-vocabulary 3D
graph systems built on posed RGB-D video or dense
maps~\cite{gu2024conceptgraphs,jatavallabhula2023conceptfusion}.

A central lesson of this work is methodological. An earlier version of this system graded its graphs
against a pseudo-reference \emph{seeded from the model's own dense-view output} --- a circular protocol
under which a method that reproduces its dense graph scores $1.0$ by construction. Re-grading against an
\emph{independently authored} reference, written from the frames, changes two conclusions. First, a
mask-plus-CLIP front-end recovers almost none of the real objects (on a desk it returns
``floor''/``curtain''/``bed'' rather than the monitors and keyboard present); a genuine
open-vocabulary \emph{detector} (OWLv2) makes the labels real. Second, a previously promising
uncertainty-aware fusion gate shows \emph{no} benefit once labels are real, across a full weight
sweep --- exactly the kind of conclusion a de-circularized evaluation is meant to surface. With honest
labels, the dominant remaining error is \emph{over-counting}: one physical object survives fusion as
several same-label nodes (Figure~\ref{fig:dedup3d}). A one-line duplicate-aware merge of same-label
nodes with overlapping 3D boxes fixes it, and is the only variant whose accuracy \emph{rises} with
more views --- the opposite move from the uncertainty gate, which over-splits.

On 28 TUM RGB-D sequences~\cite{sturm2012tumrgbd} under $3/5/8/10$ views, against an independent
reference (5 human-verified, 23 two-pass drafted), duplicate-aware fusion improves object-label F1 over
the graph-fusion baseline by $+0.06$ to $+0.12$ and wins on $22$--$27$ of $28$ scenes ($p<0.001$),
while the uncertainty gate is consistently worse. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A detector-grounded open-vocabulary sparse-view 3D scene-graph system} (OWLv2 $+$ VGGT,
  all models frozen, no per-scene optimization), whose detector labels recover real objects where a
  mask-plus-CLIP front-end does not.
  \item \textbf{Duplicate-aware cross-view fusion}: a post-fusion merge of same-label nodes whose
  axis-aligned 3D boxes overlap, targeting the measured over-counting failure; it is the only variant
  whose accuracy rises with more views.
  \item \textbf{An independent, de-circularized benchmark on TUM RGB-D}: we document how a
  prediction-seeded reference inflates scores and show that an independently authored one removes the
  artifact.
  \item \textbf{A rigorous negative result on uncertainty-aware fusion}, with a mechanism analysis ---
  it lifts recall but over-splits, costing precision --- that also explains why merging works.
\end{itemize}
